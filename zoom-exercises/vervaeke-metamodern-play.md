# Zoom Exercise Ideas: Metamodern Play

**Reference:** [Metamodern Play - John Vervaeke, Daniel GÃ¶rtz, Sarah Perry, and Laurence Currie-Clark](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iAeVE5D4tqU)
**Topic:** Dialogue on play in metamodern context
**Duration:** 90 minutes video

## Core Themes
- Play in the context of meaning crisis
- Integration of modern, postmodern, and metamodern perspectives
- Play as participatory knowing
- Relationship between play, ritual, and meaning
- Flow states and optimal grip
- Play as sacred practice
- Recovering play after postmodern irony

---

## Exercise 1: Three Ages of Play (40 minutes)

### Concept
Understand play through premodern, modern, postmodern, and metamodern lenses

### Activity
1. **Framework Presentation (10 min):** Facilitator explains:
   - **Premodern:** Play as ritual, sacred, communal
   - **Modern:** Play as development, learning, productive
   - **Postmodern:** Play as ironic, subversive, meaningless
   - **Metamodern:** Play as meaning-making, participatory, integrative

2. **Era Exploration (20 min):** Breakout rooms by era (4 groups)
   - Each explores play from their assigned perspective
   - What is play for?
   - What does it enable?
   - What are limits of this view?
   - Prepare 3-minute presentation

3. **Dialogue (10 min):** Representatives from each era discuss
   - What does each perspective offer?
   - What does each miss?
   - How might metamodern integrate them?

### Key Question
Can we recover premodern depth without losing modern/postmodern insights?

---

## Exercise 2: Participatory Knowing Through Play (35 minutes)

### Concept
Vervaeke emphasizes play as participatory knowing, not just propositional

### Activity
1. **Three Ways of Knowing (15 min):**
   - **Propositional (5 min):** Learn facts about something (lecture-style)
   - **Procedural (5 min):** Learn how to do something (follow instructions)
   - **Participatory (5 min):** Play with something (no instructions, just engage)
   - Same subject, three approaches

2. **Comparison (15 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - Which kind of knowing felt different?
   - What did each reveal?
   - Which involved play?
   - Vervaeke: Play is participatory knowing
   - What does this mean?
   - How is this different from just "learning"?

3. **Application (5 min):** Full group
   - Where do we overemphasize propositional knowing?
   - How to increase participatory knowing?
   - What role does play have?

### Key Insight
Play is a way of knowing, not just doing

---

## Exercise 3: Optimal Grip Through Play (30 minutes)

### Concept
Explore Vervaeke's concept of "optimal grip" in play context

### Activity
1. **Concept Introduction (5 min):** Facilitator explains:
   - Optimal grip: Right relationship to reality
   - Not too close (absorption), not too far (alienation)
   - Play as practice finding optimal grip

2. **Grip Experiments (15 min):**
   - **Too Tight (5 min):** Over-control an activity, micro-manage
   - **Too Loose (5 min):** Completely hands-off, disengaged
   - **Optimal (5 min):** Playful engagement, responsive
   - Notice how each feels

3. **Discussion (10 min):** Breakout rooms of 3
   - When did you find optimal grip?
   - How does play help calibrate grip?
   - Where in life is your grip off?
   - What would playful adjustment look like?

### Application
Play as practice for right relationship to reality

---

## Exercise 4: Flow and Play (35 minutes)

### Concept
Examine relationship between flow states and play

### Activity
1. **Flow Memory (8 min):** Individual reflection
   - Recall a flow state experience
   - Describe it in detail
   - Was it play?
   - What enabled it?

2. **Play vs. Flow (20 min):** Breakout rooms of 4
   - Compare flow and play:
     - Similarities?
     - Differences?
     - Can you have one without the other?
     - Flow without play? (grinding work)
     - Play without flow? (frustrated play)
   - What's the relationship?
   - Vervaeke: Both involve optimal grip

3. **Cultivation (7 min):** Return to full group
   - How to create conditions for flow-play?
   - What prevents it?
   - Can it be forced or only enabled?

### Discussion
Is flow the goal and play the method? Or vice versa?

## Exercise 5: Sacred Play (40 minutes)

### Concept
Explore play as sacred practice (Perry's contribution to dialogue)

### Activity
1. **Sacred vs. Profane (10 min):** Full group discussion
   - What makes something sacred?
   - Is play sacred or profane?
   - Modern assumption: Play is frivolous (profane)
   - Alternative: Play as holy

2. **Sacred Play Design (20 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - Design a play practice that feels sacred:
     - What elements?
     - What atmosphere?
     - What intentions?
     - What makes it sacred vs. just fun?
   - Create specific ritual-play hybrid

3. **Presentations (8 min):** Groups share designs

4. **Reflection (2 min):** Full group
   - Can play be sacred?
   - What does this even mean?
   - Metamodern integration of ritual and play

### Key Question
What would sacred play look like after loss of traditional religion?

---

## Exercise 6: Play and the Meaning Crisis (35 minutes)

### Concept
Connect play deficit to Vervaeke's meaning crisis

### Activity
1. **Meaning Crisis Summary (5 min):** Facilitator explains:
   - Modern loss of meaning-making frameworks
   - Rise of nihilism, irony, despair
   - Need for new meaning-making practices

2. **Play as Response (20 min):** Breakout rooms of 4
   - Can play address meaning crisis?
   - How does play create meaning?
   - What kind of meaning does play provide?
   - Different from religious meaning?
   - Different from achievement meaning?
   - What are limits of play for meaning?
   - Document in shared doc

3. **Synthesis (10 min):** Return to full group
   - Share key insights
   - Facilitator maps on whiteboard
   - Is play enough for meaning?
   - What else is needed?

### Discussion
Can we play our way out of the meaning crisis?

## Exercise 7: Irony, Sincerity, and Play (30 minutes)

### Concept
Navigate between postmodern irony and premodern sincerity through play

### Activity
1. **Three Modes (15 min):** Sequential experiences
   - **Ironic Mode (5 min):** Everything is joke, nothing matters
   - **Sincere Mode (5 min):** Everything is deeply serious
   - **Playful Mode (5 min):** Dancing between irony and sincerity
   - Notice how each feels

2. **Analysis (12 min):** Breakout rooms of 3
   - Is play a third mode?
   - Or synthesis of irony and sincerity?
   - How does it differ from each?
   - Metamodern move: Both ironic and sincere
   - Does play embody this?

3. **Application (3 min):** Full group
   - How to live playfully rather than ironically or naively?

### Key Insight
Play may transcend irony/sincerity dichotomy

---

## Exercise 8: Collective Meaning-Making Through Play (35 minutes)

### Concept
Experience play as shared meaning-making practice

### Activity
1. **Shared Play Creation (20 min):** Full group
   - Collaboratively create shared play-ritual:
     - Someone starts with gesture/sound
     - Next person builds on it
     - Collectively develop pattern
     - Let meaning emerge
     - Don't plan, discover through play

2. **Reflection (12 min):** Breakout rooms of 4
   - What meaning emerged?
   - How did it emerge?
   - Did anyone plan it?
   - How is this different from designed meaning?
   - Participatory vs. imposed meaning

3. **Synthesis (3 min):** Full group
   - What did we learn about collective meaning-making?

### Key Learning
Play can generate shared meaning without traditional authority

## Exercise 9: Zoom as Meaning-Making Space (30 minutes)

### Concept
Can digital space support meaningful play?

### Activity
1. **Limitations Assessment (10 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - What's harder about play on Zoom?
   - What's missing from embodied play?
   - Can Zoom support participatory knowing?
   - What are affordances vs. limitations?

2. **Design Challenge (15 min):** Continue in groups
   - How to maximize meaningful play on Zoom?
   - Specific features to use?
   - Specific practices?
   - Ways to compensate for limitations?
   - Create concrete proposals

3. **Sharing (5 min):** Quick presentations

### Question
Is digital play adequate for meaning-making or just substitute?

## Exercise 10: Integration Practice Design (40 minutes)

### Concept
Design ongoing metamodern play practices for this group

### Activity
1. **Principle Review (5 min):** Facilitator summarizes dialogue themes:
   - Participatory knowing
   - Optimal grip
   - Sacred play
   - Flow states
   - Meaning-making
   - Integration of perspectives

2. **Practice Design (25 min):** Breakout rooms of 4-5
   - Create specific practices for this Zoom group:
     - How to embody participatory knowing?
     - How to cultivate optimal grip?
     - How to honor sacred dimension?
     - How to enable flow?
     - How to generate shared meaning?
   - Must be concrete and repeatable
   - Document in shared doc

3. **Presentations (8 min):** Each group shares

4. **Adoption (2 min):** Vote on practices to implement

### Outcome
Concrete metamodern play practices

## Facilitator Notes

### Preparation
- Watch the 90-minute dialogue (maybe multiple times)
- Familiarize with Vervaeke's broader work on meaning crisis
- Understand metamodernism basics
- Consider your own relationship to meaning and play

### Managing Complexity
- This is philosophically sophisticated material
- Some will find it abstract
- Ground constantly in experience
- Use embodiment and practice
- Allow for different levels of engagement

### Holding Multiple Perspectives
- Metamodernism is about integration, not synthesis
- Hold tensions rather than resolving them
- Model both/and thinking
- Avoid simplification

### Connection to Reading List
- Culmination of ritual section
- Integrates previous readings
- Adds meaning dimension to play
- Connects to phenomenology section
- Prepares for serious play section
