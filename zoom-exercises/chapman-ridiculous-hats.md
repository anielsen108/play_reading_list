# Zoom Exercise Ideas: On Wearing Ridiculous Hats

**Reference:** [On Wearing Ridiculous Hats - David Chapman](https://twitter.com/Meaningness/status/1369146971181576195)
**Topic:** Loss of playfulness in everyday life
**Duration:** 5 minutes reading time (Twitter thread)

## Core Themes
- Historical playfulness in everyday dress and comportment
- Modern loss of whimsy, ritual, and performance
- Seriousness and uniformity as modern default
- Playfulness as cultural health indicator
- Recovering everyday play

---

## Exercise 1: Ridiculous Accessory Session (20 minutes)

### Concept
Actually wear something ridiculous on Zoom

### Activity
1. **Preparation (5 min):** Everyone finds something playful to wear
   - Hat, wig, mask, costume piece, silly accessory
   - Or use Zoom filters/effects creatively

2. **Performance (10 min):** Full group, cameras on
   - Everyone appears in their ridiculous items
   - Conduct normal discussion
   - Notice: How does playful appearance affect interaction?

3. **Reflection (5 min):**
   - How did it feel to be ridiculous?
   - Did it change the conversation?
   - What prevents this normally?
   - Chapman's point: We've lost this everyday playfulness

### Key Insight
Small playful elements can transform entire atmosphere

---

## Exercise 2: Historical Playfulness Research (30 minutes)

### Concept
Investigate how people historically incorporated play into daily life

### Activity
1. **Research Assignment (15 min):** Breakout rooms by era:
   - Group 1: Medieval/Renaissance
   - Group 2: 18th/19th century
   - Group 3: Early 20th century
   - Group 4: Mid-20th century
   - Quick research: How did people play in everyday life?
   - Dress, festivals, public performance, games

2. **Timeline Creation (12 min):** Return to full group
   - Each group presents findings
   - Facilitator creates timeline on whiteboard
   - Notice: When did playfulness decline?

3. **Analysis (3 min):**
   - What changed?
   - Why did we lose everyday playfulness?
   - Chapman: Modernization as de-playification

### Discussion
When did life become so serious?

---

## Exercise 3: Play Audit of Daily Life (25 minutes)

### Concept
Examine how much playfulness exists in ordinary activities

### Activity
1. **Typical Day Analysis (10 min):** Individual, cameras off
   - Walk through typical day
   - What's playful vs. serious/uniform?
   - Dress, movement, speech, interaction
   - Rate: 1-10 for playfulness

2. **Comparison (12 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - Share findings
   - Common patterns:
     - Work: Serious
     - Home: Slightly more playful?
     - Public: Uniform
     - Digital: ???
   - How much playfulness overall?
   - Would historical person find our lives playful or dead?

3. **Group Insight (3 min):**
   - Average playfulness score
   - What prevents more?

### Reflection
How play-less is modern life?

---

## Exercise 4: Zoom Backgrounds as Play (20 minutes)

### Concept
Explore virtual backgrounds as modern playful expression

### Activity
1. **Background Play (10 min):** Full group
   - Everyone creates/chooses ridiculous virtual background
   - More absurd the better
   - Present yourself in silly setting
   - Conduct normal discussion

2. **Analysis (8 min):** Breakout rooms of 3
   - Is this playful or just technological?
   - Does it serve same function as ridiculous hats?
   - What's different about digital play?
   - Does it count?

3. **Synthesis (2 min):** Full group
   - Can technology restore playfulness?
   - Or is it a substitute?

### Question
Is digital playfulness the same as embodied playfulness?

---

## Exercise 5: Playful Speech Experiment (25 minutes)

### Concept
Bring playfulness into language and speech patterns

### Activity
1. **Normal Speech (5 min):** Breakout rooms
   - Discuss topic normally
   - Notice tone, vocabulary, patterns

2. **Playful Speech (8 min):** Same groups
   - Same topic, but with:
     - Made-up words
     - Silly voices
     - Exaggerations
     - Wordplay
     - Nonsense interjections

3. **Comparison (10 min):** Return to full group
   - Which was more engaging?
   - Which was more memorable?
   - Did playful speech hinder communication?
   - What did it add?
   - Historical note: Much more linguistic play in past

4. **Reflection (2 min):**
   - Why is modern speech so uniform?

### Key Learning
Playful language creates different quality of connection

---

## Exercise 6: The Seriousness Police (30 minutes)

### Concept
Notice how playfulness is socially policed

### Activity
1. **Observation Inventory (10 min):** Individual reflection
   - When have you been told to "be serious"?
   - When have you policed someone else's playfulness?
   - What gets sanctioned?
   - What's acceptable?
   - Write examples

2. **Pattern Analysis (15 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - Share examples
   - Who polices? (authority, peers, self?)
   - Where is playfulness least tolerated?
   - What's the punishment?
   - What's the rationale?
   - Is it justified?

3. **Resistance (5 min):** Full group
   - How to resist seriousness police?
   - When to comply vs. defy?
   - Group pact to allow playfulness?

### Discussion
Are we all complicit in killing play?

---

## Exercise 7: Festival Recovery (35 minutes)

### Concept
Chapman references loss of festivals and public play. Imagine recovery.

### Activity
1. **Historical Festivals (10 min):** Facilitator presents:
   - Carnival
   - May Day
   - Fool's Day
   - Seasonal celebrations
   - All involved public playfulness

2. **Modern Equivalent Design (20 min):** Breakout rooms of 4-5
   - Design a modern festival for your community:
     - What playful elements?
     - Who participates?
     - What rules are suspended?
     - What's the point?
     - How frequent?
   - Make it concrete and doable
   - Address obstacles

3. **Presentations (5 min):** Quick shares from groups

### Application
Could we actually create playful public rituals?

---

## Exercise 8: Performativity vs. Authenticity (25 minutes)

### Concept
Chapman contrasts playful performance with modern authenticity demands

### Activity
1. **Authenticity Discussion (10 min):** Breakout rooms of 3
   - Modern value: "Just be yourself"
   - Historical value: "Perform your role playfully"
   - Which is better?
   - What does each enable/prevent?
   - Can you be both authentic and performative?

2. **Experiment (10 min):**
   - Round 1 (5 min): Be "authentically yourself"
   - Round 2 (5 min): Perform a playful character
   - Which felt freer?

3. **Reflection (5 min):** Full group
   - Paradox: Performance might enable freedom
   - Authenticity might be constraining
   - Chapman: We lost something valuable

## Exercise 9: Everyday Play Injection (30 minutes)

### Concept
Identify specific ways to inject play into mundane activities

### Activity
1. **Activity Selection (5 min):** Full group
   - List mundane daily activities:
     - Meetings
     - Meals
     - Commuting
     - Chores
     - Email
     - Etc.

2. **Playful Redesign (18 min):** Breakout rooms of 4
   - Choose 3-4 activities
   - For each, design playful version:
     - What playful element to add?
     - How to implement?
     - What's the barrier?
     - What's the benefit?
   - Be specific and practical

3. **Sharing (5 min):** Quick round of best ideas

4. **Commitment (2 min):** Each person commits to one

### Outcome
Concrete practices to try

---

## Exercise 10: Zoom Culture Playfulness (20 minutes)

### Concept
Make this Zoom group more playful going forward

### Activity
1. **Current State (5 min):** Full group assessment
   - How playful is this group currently?
   - Rate 1-10
   - What prevents more playfulness?

2. **Design Sprint (12 min):** Breakout rooms of 3-4
   - How to make Zoom sessions more playful?
   - Specific ideas:
     - Dress/appearance
     - Speech patterns
     - Rituals
     - Activities
     - Norms
   - What would Chapman approve of?

3. **Adoption (3 min):** Full group
   - Vote on practices to implement
   - Commit to trying them

### Goal
Actually change group culture
